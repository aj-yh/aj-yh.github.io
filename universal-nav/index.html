Alex Hayashi

Creating “Universal Navigation” for the Web experience

Role

  • Drove cross-team alignment and stakeholder buy-in to move project forward
  • Collaborated with product and engineering leaders to plan and execute a seamless rollout
  • Provided ongoing design feedback and shaped the overall direction

Credit goes to Laura Kennedy for her work on this project. The final UI/UX and documentation are the product of her hard work!

Context

Over several years, GoodRx had accumulated 50+ different variations of page header and footer navigations, leading to a fragmented, disorienting experience when moving throughout the Web experience. While most people in the organization agreed that this wasn’t an ideal situation, there was not a clear path to solving the problem. Various designers have worked on versions of the project on the side, but without organizational investment, building a truly “universal” navigation and rolling it out was not going to happen.

Getting initial buy-in

The most compelling argument for the project was actually one of the most simple. We created a recording showing how the page header was noticeably different after clicking on each element within the header navigation. With this visceral illustration of the issue as a foundation, we also connected the project to key organizational goals. These included the desire to drive account sign-ups and logins, as well as cross-site movement from editorial content to our prescription savings offerings. This combination of factors made the case for this effort to become a formal roadmap initiative.

Evolving alongside the organization

Getting alignment on the information architecture (IA) of the navigation from teams across the organization was a matter of active communication. We diagrammed out a proposed IA based on earlier design work, made intentional edits, and then met with representatives from the 5+ teams who owned a section of the navigation. Through live conversation and principled compromise, we were able to align on an IA that worked for everyone.

Design, build, rollout, evolve

Building on the framework set out by designers who worked on previous proposals for this project, we aligned on a scalable model that could adjust to support the organization as its priorities continued to shift. We worked to refine the design across every possible breakpoint and device size, ensuring the experience could meet every user where they were.

Through careful planning and coordination across multiple teams, we updated nearly every page (excluding truly legacy ones) in the GoodRx Web experience to use the new Universal Navigation component. Over time, it has continued to evolve to support new product launches, changes in organizational metrics, and the evolution of our visual language.

Impact

The initial launch and subsequent evolution of the Universal Navigation has delivered meaningful qualitative and quantitative benefits, including:

  • Improved site-wide UX through a consistent navigation experience that meets users wherever they are, providing a way to navigate through all our offerings regardless of what acquisition channel they initially came through.
  • Provided high-visibility entry points for key features like search and new company offerings like conditions subscriptions.
  • Drove improved organic traffic and key page ranking through intentional, SEO-focused improvements within the component.
  • An initial launch test, on the home page, drove an 8–9% increase in member registration and account creation (~100–110k individuals), with a 22% increase in annual logins (205k individuals) within a month.
