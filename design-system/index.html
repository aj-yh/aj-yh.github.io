Alex Hayashi

Building and evolving the design system

Role

  • Defined overall strategy and process for the team transition, creating accountability to a higher bar for quality and collaboration
  • Directly contributed design and engineering work alongside members of the Platform Design, Frontend Platform, and Mobile App Platform teams

Note: Credit goes to Laura Kennedy, Faye Xu, and Jay Lucien for their work on the design system & its support structure

Context

When I joined GoodRx, the Design System team was in a complicated position. While libraries existed in code and Figma for Web, iOS, and Android, there were some fundamental issues. Code and Figma components were not in sync, documentation was spotty, and the system lacked a general sense of coherence.

Moreover, there was a lack of trust between the designers and engineers in the organization and the Design System team. This lead to spotty adoption of the design system, unhealthy tension between teams, and an unclear future for the Design System team. Overall, the organization was not getting the full benefit of having a design system and dedicated team supporting it.

Strengthening the foundations

In order to get the organization the velocity, quality, and scalability benefits of a design system, the system and the team managing it needed intentional changes. These took the form of a wide set of tangible and intangible tactics, including:

  • Making sure that using the design system was the easiest way for teams to build their experiences with velocity and quality. We created consistency between design & code, delivered easy-to-use documentation, and prioritized work to support the needs of other teams.
  • Shifting the fundamental framing of how we worked, from "setting the standards" to enabling delivery for other teams. We adopted a communication style based in collaboration rather than governance, and focused on support for the wider organization, e.g. making ourselves available to provide active support to designers and engineers through Office Hours, Slack support channels, and more.
  • Building trust in the system was by ensuring it reflected the work of the whole organization, not just the team in charge of managing it. We worked closely with designers and engineers to evolve the system based on their needs, adding new components, variants, and configurations to support new launches, while still maintaining system cohesion. We even drove direct contributions to the design system by other teams.

These changes, alongside of host of others, drove increased adoption across every product team. They also radically altered the way other teams interacted with us, turning us into proactive partners on new work, rather than a begrudging checkpoint.

Evolving alongside the organization

With widespread adoption, the design system could now be a catalyst for organization wide change. The system has been through a variety of changes including an initial visual rebrand, a ground up technical rebuild, and then even further visual refinement. Each shift offered an opportunity to improve the quality, usability, and accessibility of what we offered. In practice, this took the form of:

  • As part of the rollout of the initial visual rebrand, we rebuilt the Figma library from the ground up, implementing the latest component features, and vastly improving the Figma experience for designers around the organization. We also iterated on top of the initial recommendations that had come from an outside agency, ensuring accessibility across our new visual language. The rollout of the rebrand was then effectively a "switch flip" across the Web and Mobile App experiences.
  • As a part of the ground up technical rebuild, we made our components more composable and configurable, making it far easier for teams to build with, and enabling quicker iteration and expansion on what the system had to offer. The rebuilt design system was the proving ground for a new technical architecture, and set the example for how other teams should build.
  • As part of the most recent round of visual refinements, we led the effort modernize our UI style, supporting the rollout of new product offerings and brand campaigns. Through close partnership with product and brand marketing teams, we were able to deliver system-level improvements as the needs emerged, and in advance of projects being built.

Building partnerships and capabilities

As the design system and the team matured, we started to pursue active partnerships with other teams to embed systems thinking into feature work. This was done with the intent of driving scalability and velocity beyond the foundational design system elements. Two example of this were:

  • Partnering with the team focused on GoodRx's new condition subscription offerings. This collaboration involved updating design system elements to meet their needs and helping them build more complex reusable components to power key facets of their experience, like their intake flows and subscriber dashboard experience.
  • Partnering with the Payments team to standardize payment and address entry and selection experiences. This involved auditing existing implementations and then building standard components to consolidate the current solutions. The team then also lead to work to update the account-level payment method and address management experiences, enabling key capabilities across all teams.

Impact

Through our ongoing efforts to ensure the design system, and the experience of working with it, are optimal, we’ve achieved meaningful results:

  • Across Web, iOS, and Android, all launches by all teams utilize design system elements as the foundation of their UI. Precise coverage numbers vary on whether a launch involves introducing new UI/UX paradigms, or works exclusively with existing pieces.
  • We build proactive partnerships with teams around the organization to add to or update the design system to support emerging needs, in advance of the actual feature work beginning.
  • We’re meaningfully embedded in team critiques, providing systems guidance when appropriate. The team’s input is sought out in strategic and tactical conversations to help inform how problems are solved.
